What to do when you see a snake

Stay calm and create space. Most snakebite incidents happen when people try to catch, kill, or move the snake themselves.

1

Stop and step back

Move slowly away. Keep at least several meters of distance and do not corner the snake.

2

Clear the area

Take children, elderly people, pets, and bystanders away from the room, garden, vehicle, or stairway.

3

Observe safely

If possible, watch where the snake goes from a safe distance. Do not poke it or throw objects.

4

Call rescue

Call Mohammed Akhil and share your exact location, the snake's last position, and any nearby risks.

If someone is bitten

  • Seek emergency medical care immediately.
  • Keep the person calm, seated or lying down, and still.
  • Remove rings, watches, or tight clothing before swelling starts.
  • Take a photo of the snake only from a safe distance, if possible.
  • Cover the bite with a clean, dry dressing while waiting for help.

Do not do these

  • Do not try to catch, kill, or handle the snake.
  • Do not cut the wound or suck out venom.
  • Do not apply ice, electric shock, or folk remedies.
  • Do not use a tourniquet or tightly tie the limb.
  • Do not drink alcohol or delay hospital treatment.

Snakes are safest when handled by trained rescuers

Whether the snake is in a garden, parking area, home, shop, or school, keep the space quiet and wait for trained help.

Precautions around home and work

Simple habits reduce surprise encounters and make rescue easier if a snake appears.

Keep surroundings clear

Remove unused bricks, wood piles, tall grass, and clutter where snakes or rodents may hide.

Control food sources

Store grains and waste properly. Rodents attract snakes, especially near houses and shops.

Use lights at night

Carry a torch in dark areas, staircases, gardens, parking spaces, and near drains.

Wear protection

Use shoes, long pants, and gloves when clearing brush, debris, or storage rooms.

Seal entry points

Close gaps below doors, broken vents, drain openings, and holes near compound walls.

Teach children

Tell children never to touch a snake, even if it looks small, slow, or injured.

FAQs

Quick answers for common snake rescue situations in homes, shops, schools, and outdoor areas.

Should I try to catch the snake before the rescuer arrives?

No. Keep distance and watch from a safe place if possible. Trying to catch or kill the snake can cause a bite and makes rescue harder.

Can I send a photo for identification?

Yes, only if you can take it safely from far away. Do not move closer for a better picture.

What should I tell on the phone?

Share your name, exact address, nearest landmark, where the snake is now, whether anyone is bitten, and whether pets or children are nearby.

What if the snake disappears?

Do not search with bare hands. Close the room or area if safe, keep people away, and tell the rescuer the last seen location.

Are all snakes dangerous?

No, many snakes are non-venomous and help control rodents. Still, treat every unknown snake with caution and call a trained rescuer.

Do you protect the snake too?

Yes. The goal is public safety and humane rescue, so the animal is handled with care and moved away from conflict whenever possible.
